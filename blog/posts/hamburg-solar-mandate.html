Thesis Update

Getting Started: Measuring Hamburg's Solar Mandate

May 2026 · 5 min read

My bachelor's thesis asks a deceptively simple question: did Hamburg's solar roof mandate actually cause more solar panels to be installed? It sounds straightforward — compare before and after, check if installations went up. But doing this rigorously turns out to be genuinely hard.

The policy

In January 2023, Hamburg became the first German state to mandate solar panels on private residential buildings — not just commercial ones. Most other states either don't mandate private buildings at all, or only introduced that requirement years later. A second wave of the mandate hit in January 2024, extending the obligation to substantial roof renovations (Dachsanierungen).

On paper, this should be an empiricist's dream: a clear treatment, a clear date, and a comparison group of nearby regions without the same mandate. In practice, it's messier.

The identification problem

The core challenge is the EEG reform of 2023. Around the same time Hamburg's mandate came into force, the German federal government removed VAT on residential solar systems and simplified the grid connection rules. The result was a nationwide solar boom — installations roughly tripled across the country. Hamburg went up by 2.9×; the surrounding control regions went up by 3.2×.

That already hints at something interesting (Hamburg grew less than its neighbours in percentage terms, despite the mandate), but the raw comparison is complicated by the fact that Hamburg had already been accelerating before 2023. Separating the mandate's effect from the national tailwind is the central econometric problem of the thesis.

The design

I'm using a staggered difference-in-differences approach. The treatment unit is Hamburg; the control group is six surrounding Landkreise — four in Schleswig-Holstein (Pinneberg, Segeberg, Stormarn, Herzogtum Lauenburg) and two in Niedersachsen (Harburg, Stade). I observe them at the PLZ × month level from 2019 onwards, which gives me enough pre-treatment data to test parallel trends.

One useful feature of the control group: Schleswig-Holstein introduced a commercial-only mandate in January 2023. That means commercial installations in SH serve as a built-in placebo — if my estimator picks up a treatment effect for private buildings in Hamburg but not for commercial buildings in SH, that's reassuring. If it fires on both, something is off.

The data

The main data source is the Marktstammdatenregister (MaStR), Germany's national plant register. Every solar installation has to be registered there within a few months of going live. I'm using the commissioning date (Inbetriebnahmedatum), not the registration date, since registration lags average about 17% above one year — using the wrong date would systematically misallocate installations to the wrong month.

One thing I didn't expect: the explosion of balcony solar panels (Balkonkraftwerke) after 2023. These tiny plug-in units are registered in MaStR like any other installation, which inflates private installation counts dramatically. I filter them out using a 0.8 kW threshold in the regional data and an explicit category flag in the national data.

What's next

The descriptive analysis is mostly done. The next step is specifying the regression model — I'm deciding between a standard two-way fixed effects estimator and a more robust staggered estimator like Callaway-Sant'Anna, which handles heterogeneous treatment effects across time better. Given that I have two treatment dates (2023 and 2024), the staggered approach is probably the right call.

More updates to follow as the results come in.
