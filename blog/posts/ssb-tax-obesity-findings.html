Do Sugar Taxes Reduce Childhood Obesity? What the Data Says

March 2024 · 5 min read

Taxes on sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) are one of the most widely discussed tools in public health policy. Over 85 countries have some form of SSB tax in place, and the WHO actively encourages others to follow. The underlying logic is intuitive: make sugary drinks more expensive, people drink less of them, and obesity rates fall. But does the data actually support this?

For my Global Health Politics term paper, I ran a difference-in-differences analysis to find out. Here are the key findings.

The setup

The UK and Ireland introduced SSB taxes in 2018. This gave me a natural experiment: two countries with a new policy intervention, and two comparable countries without one — Germany and Switzerland — that serve as the control group. Using WHO Global Health Observatory data on obesity prevalence among children and adolescents aged 5–19, I tracked all four countries from 2000 to 2022 (n = 92 country-year observations).

The DiD framework compares the change in obesity rates in the treatment countries (UK, Ireland) before and after 2018, against the change over the same period in the control countries (Germany, Switzerland). If the tax had an effect, we would expect the treatment group to diverge downward from the control group after 2018.

Validating the design

Before interpreting the results, I needed to check whether the DiD design was valid. The core assumption is parallel trends: that treatment and control countries were on similar obesity trajectories before the tax came in.

Assumption holds

The parallel trends test returned p = 0.773, giving no reason to reject the assumption. The pre-treatment obesity trends across all four countries were statistically indistinguishable.

This matters: if the trends had already been diverging before 2018, any post-2018 difference could not be cleanly attributed to the tax.

The main result

The DiD regression estimated the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATET) — that is, the additional change in obesity prevalence in the UK and Ireland that can be attributed to the SSB tax, relative to what would have happened without it.

Main finding — null result

ATET = −0.181 pp  ·  p = 0.38

The estimated effect points in the right direction — a small reduction in obesity prevalence — but it is not statistically significant. The data cannot rule out that this effect is simply noise.

A Granger causality test confirmed this: there was no significant evidence (p = 0.233) that changes in obesity rates followed from the introduction of SSB taxes rather than from other concurrent trends.

Why might this be?

A null result doesn't mean the tax did nothing — it means the study couldn't detect an effect. Several factors could explain the gap between theory and findings:

Tax level. The UK's soft drinks levy charges between 18p and 24p per litre depending on sugar content. Modelling studies suggest that much steeper taxes (around 20% price increases) may be needed to shift behaviour enough to affect obesity rates at the population level.

Complementary policies. SSB taxes rarely operate in isolation. Their effectiveness likely depends on accompanying measures — nutritional education, restrictions on marketing to children, improved access to healthier food. Without these, the isolated effect of a tax may be too small to detect.

Time horizon. Obesity is a slow-moving outcome. The tax was introduced in 2018 and my data runs to 2022 — just four years of post-treatment observations. Population-level changes in BMI may take a decade or more to materialise in the data.

Substitution effects. Consumers may compensate by increasing caloric intake elsewhere. If a family switches from Coke to cheaper, equally caloric fruit juices or snacks, the net effect on obesity could be negligible.

What this doesn't mean

A null result in one study is not evidence that SSB taxes are ineffective. Other research — including Rogers et al. (2023) on the UK levy specifically — does find associations with reduced obesity prevalence in younger school children. My study covered the 5–19 age group in aggregate, which may mask heterogeneous effects across different sub-groups.

The findings are also constrained by the small number of countries (four) and the reliance on BMI as the outcome measure — BMI is a useful proxy but imperfectly captures body composition, especially in growing adolescents.

The bottom line

SSB taxes are a promising and relatively easy-to-implement policy instrument, but the evidence that they reduce childhood obesity on their own, within a short timeframe, is not yet conclusive. The most defensible interpretation is that they are one necessary piece of a broader strategy — not a standalone solution.

Whether they work better in combination with other interventions, over longer timescales, or at higher tax rates are exactly the questions future research should focus on.

This post summarises findings from a term paper submitted for Global Health Politics at the University of Hamburg (March 2024, supervisor: Prof. Dr. Max Schaub). Data source: WHO Global Health Observatory. Analysis conducted in Stata 18.
